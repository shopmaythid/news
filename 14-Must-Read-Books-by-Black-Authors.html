14 Must Read Books by Black Authors

By: Hayley Dennings

My name is Hayley Dennings. I am a junior at Loyola Marymount University in Los Angeles and an active participant in an online book community, mostly on Twitter and YouTube. The murder of George Floyd pushed members of this online book community to be more inclusive towards black creators and authors. One of my friends on Twitter challenged her followers, and anyone else that saw her tweet, to post books featuring black characters or written by black authors. Accepting the challenge, I pulled all of the books authored by black people from my shelves and posted them, encouraging my friends and followers to educate themselves on the black experience. Some of those books I have read and loved, some of those books I plan on reading in order to support the authors and their stories. These are those books.

The Books That I Have Read & That You Should Read, Too
PreviousNext

Children of Blood and Bone is a 2018 debut young adult fantasy novel written by Nigerian American novelist, Tomi Adeyemi. This is Adeymi’s first book in the trilogy. The story follows the protagonist, Zelie Adebola as she embarks on a journey to restore magic to the kingdom of Orisha. Zelie belongs to the suppressed group of magic practitioners called the maji. Adeyemi drew inspiration from West African Mythology and the Yoruba culture with this book. The story also portrays the theme of abuse of power and oppression, derived from Adeyemi’s experience with police brutality towards African American people. Adeyemi paints a vivid picture that examines real issues such as oppression, racism, and slavery within a coming of age adventure story that features an all black cast of characters.

The Hate U Give is a 2017 debut contemporary young adult novel written by African American author, Angie Thomas.  The novel was expanded from a short story Thomas wrote in college as a reaction to the murder of Oscar Grant. The story features a 16 year old black girl, Starr Carter, who witnesses the police shooting of her childhood friend. When the story hits the national news, she becomes a passionate member of the fight to get justice for her friend.

Everything Everything is a debut contemporary young adult novel by Jamaican American author, Nicola Yoon. The story follows 17 year old Madeline Whittier, who suffers from severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID), which is also called “bubble baby disease”. Her illness forces her to remain indoors 24/7, never to go outside, and limited to interaction with a select group of people. When Madeline takes interest in Oliver, her new nextdoor neighbor, her acceptance of her illness crumples and she finds herself willing to take risks to live a normal life. 

Parable of the Sower is a 1993 science fiction novel written by African American novelist, Octavia E. Butler.  It is the first book in the Earthseed duology. The story takes place in the 2020s, within a corrupt and broken society, damaged by climate change, wealth inequality, and corporate greed. The protagonist, Lauren Oya Olamina has an ability called hyperempathy, that makes her feel the sensations that she witnesses other people and animals experiencing. She grows up in a gated compound near Los Angeles, but must flee north with other survivors when it gets destroyed. Resource scarcity and poverty plague society outside of the walls, and Lauren must navigate through it all to find safety.

The New Jim Crow is a nonfiction book by Michelle Alexander, an African-American civil rights litigator and legal scholar. This book examines racial issues pertaining to African American males and mass incarceration in the United States. Alexander’s main argument states that “mass incarceration is, metaphorically, the New Jim Crow”. She states how the U.S. criminal justice system uses the War on Drugs as a way to perpetuate discrimination and oppression. The system has created a disproportionately high rate of incarceration for African American men, thus making them the primary targets of the system increasingly defined by race.

Brown Girl Dreaming is a 2014 teen novel told in verse by African American author, Jacqueline Woodson. The story depicts Woodson’s experience growing up as an African American in the 1960s and 1970s, amongst the fading Jim Crow era and the expanding Civil Rights Movement. This book has been awarded the National Book Award for Young People’s Literature, the Coretta Scott King Book Award, and an NAACP Image Award for outstanding literary work.

Citizen is a 2014 “book-length” poem written by African American poet, Claudia Rankine. This book combines multiple forms of writing and media into a collection that describes race relations in the United States. Rankine critiques racism and visibility while examining the microaggressions that African Americans experience everyday. While discussing controversial incidents such as the murder of Trayvon Martin and the criticism against Serena Williams, she includes mesmerizing and provoking images in an attempt to give visibility to the black experience.

Passing is a 1929 novel written by African American author, Nella Larsen. The story takes place in Harlem, New York during the 1920s, following the protagonist, Irene Redfield. Irene’s reunion with her childhood friend, Clare Kendry is the central driving force of the story, as their fascination with each other increases. Passing examines “racial passing”, which is the main theme of this novel, as many black characters participate in passing as white in order to have a better standard of living. The novel highlights the dangers of passing as white while remaining connected to the black community. 

Beloved is a 1987 novel written by African American author, Toni Morrison. The novel follows the protagonist, Sethe, a young woman and escaped slave, who is still haunted by her traumatic past. While she is an escaped slave, she is not free because of the bonds her past has kept on her. A secret she’s been harboring for years is brought to light when the embodiment of her past and true fears shows up in the form of a teenage girl calling herself Beloved.

All American Boys is a young adult novel is a 2015 novel co-written by Jason Reynolds and Brendan Kiely. The story depicts an instance of police brutality from two different points of view. One perspective is from a high schooler, Rashad, who gets beaten by the police after being wrongfully accused of shoplifting. The other perspective is from a high schooler, Quinn, who pretends he did not see the beating. The dual perspectives, white and black, are confronted with the ugly truth of racism and prejudice.

Americanah is a 2013 novel by Nigerian author, Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie. The story follows a young Nigerian woman, Ifemelu, who travels to attend university in the United States, where she is forced to confront the realities of being black in America. Her life in Nigeria and the US are portrayed, coming together to form a whirlwind romance with her Nigerian classmate, Obinze.

The Fifth Season is a 2015 science fantasy novel written by African American author, N.K. Jemisin. It is the first book in the Broken Earth trilogy. The story follows a woman, Essun, who sets out to repair her broken family after her husband and son are brutally murdered and her daughter kidnapped. The land around her is dying, with limited access to sunlight, clean water, and food. As the resources grow more scarce, tensions between the nations increase. Essun must compete with the rest of the world in order to find her daughter.

Queen Sugar is a 2014 contemporary debut novel written by African American author, Natalie Baszille. It tells the story of Charley Bordelon, an African American widow and mother, who is struggling with the weight of taking on the sugarcane plantation left behind by her late father. She must move with her daughter from California to Louisiana and learn how to navigate an industry dominated by white men.

Their Eyes Were Watching God is a 1937 written by an African American author, Zora Neale Hurston. Throughout the novel, character Janie Crawford, an African American woman, grows from a teenage girl into a woman who will shape her own destiny. This book is considered to be a classic of the Harlem Renaissance.

Remember to buy from black owned bookstores!
