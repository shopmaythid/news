Revolution Outside My Window

By: Ariah Henderson

"To be is to fight; that sifting through entry-level jobs and dreaming of a space to call my own is a revolution of self, and that as a Black woman any iteration of peace, or personal growth or effort to find your footing in a world built to knock off your feet is revolutionary."

There is a revolution outside my window, but I am home scrolling through Craigslist, Indeed, and LinkedIn looking for jobs, bookmarking apartments slightly out of my price range with big south-facing windows, hardwood floors, and a claw foot tub. There is a revolution outside my window, but I am home on the phone with the Department of Human Services, trying to get food stamps to feed myself while I work towards a degree that will probably not help me pay the bills enough to get off of food stamps upon graduation.
 
There is a revolution outside my window, but I am trying to grow up. I have two warring ideals in my body right now. One says get up and fight this fight on the frontlines with tear gas in my eyes and smoke in my lungs. The other says to be is to fight; that sifting through entry-level jobs and dreaming of a space to call my own is a revolution of self, and that as a Black woman any iteration of peace, or personal growth or effort to find your footing in a world built to knock off your feet is revolutionary.
 
While social media has had an invaluable part of this revolution through education, news, and awareness, I think the place of Black voices has been whittled to a single narrative of the activist educator. These past weeks, I have admittedly done my fair share of educating via direct call outs and indirect Instagram stories about supporting Black businesses, reading radical texts written by Black writers, and breaking down my own privilege as a light-skinned CIS-gendered woman. But tonight I am tired.
 
The thing is, my direct and indirect education and call outs are not unique to this revolution. I have been kicking and screaming through emails, DMs, texts, and direct confrontation with racist white people weaponizing their power to perpetuate anti-Blackness since I was 14.
 
This revolution, this fight, is not new to Black folks. So, when I say that there is a revolution outside my window, but I am home scrolling through Craigslist, Indeed, and LinkedIn looking for jobs, bookmarking apartments slightly out of my price range with big south-facing windows, hardwood floors, and a claw foot tub, what I really mean is: There is a revolution outside my window, but my body is exhausted from decades of settler-colonialist capitalism chipping away at my humanity, and while the world fights, I am forced to subscribe to this generation’s edition of systematic oppression in order to put myself in a more comfortable situation, so that hopefully I can get off of food stamps and see my body, my story, represented in positions of power.
 
There is no one way to revolt, especially for Black people. Fucking up a cop and taking a bubble bath are equally radical acts of Black expression. 400 years of trauma course through our veins. Any moment of peace, no matter what that looks like is revolution.
 
So, Black folks, never feel like the way you fight be it externally or internally is not enough. I love you all the time, and I fight with you all the time even though some nights it’s from my second-hand futon in my cheap sublet room while looking for a minimum wage waitressing gig dreaming of natural light, hardwood floors, and a warm bath.
